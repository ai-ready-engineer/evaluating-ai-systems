Experiment Design and Evaluation of AI Systems

Reliable eval means efficient product cycles, informed decisions, better products, happier customers.

Open the Playground →

The course has no prerequisites except for:

  • Curiosity
  • Love for your products
  • Passion for your customers

The course is "hands-on" but no coding is required: you will get to play with simple AI agents to feel what uncertainty looks like and how it affects a system.

Who this course is for
  • Engineers and engineering managers: Iterate towards success. Understand what works, what doesn't, and where to put resources.
  • Product managers: Translate goals into measurable evals. Ship with confidence.
  • Executives (compact course): Understand what results say and don't say. Ask impactful questions that help teams grow. Promote a culture that recognizes and manages uncertainty.

How the course works

Every lesson is built the same way:

  • It has tiles describing what we cover. You can see if you are already familiar with the content, or need a refresher, or it is new for you.
  • It isolates one source of uncertainty, so you meet it on its own before it combines with the others.
  • It teaches the same three moves on it: understand it, report it, manage it.
  • It contrasts good practice against the anti-pattern that fools people — each shown through a worked example of how the mistake actually happens.
  • It hands you a hands-on playground — a web tool you first use to feel the concept, then point at your own real eval numbers.
  • It includes an optional code example, for those who want to go deeper.

Optional deep dives go further for the curious — where statistics came from, and why the normal distribution shows up.

Two course-wide tools run across every lesson: Ask the Playground (ask any page why a number moved) and the Experiment-Report Evaluator (submit a write-up, get structured feedback).

What you will learn

By the end of the course, evaluation will look like the feedback loop that lets a team improve faster, ship more often, and still ship reliable AI products.

You will be able to:

  • Design experiments that make product iteration faster and releases safer.
  • Use evals to find where quality is actually blocked.
  • Apply a practical toolkit: metrics, test sets, holdouts, breakdowns, judge checks, and re-baselining.
  • Spot the traps that make teams move fast in the wrong direction: bias, overfitting, noisy judges, drift, and too many metrics.
  • Report results in a shared language: what works, what is uncertain, what the limitations are, and what decision the evidence supports.

What the course is NOT about
  • We are not training ML models — the focus is on evaluating AI agents or already-trained models.
  • We are not covering methods for monitoring, observability, and telemetry.

The sessions

Eight 1-hour sessions. The first seven isolate one source of uncertainty at a time; the final capstone runs an end-to-end experiment design. Every session comes with totally optional hands-on labs and deep dives, for those who want to go further.

L1
"Eval" is now an Experiment

From traditional software to ML to AI agents — an eval is an experiment, not a check.

Content
  • Why "progress" means different things in software 1.0 vs. ML vs. gen AI
  • An eval as an experiment that estimates a quantity, not a deterministic check
  • Metrics, test sets, the sealed test set
  • Class imbalance and test-set stratification — why a "95% accurate" classifier might just be predicting the majority class every time
  • Why a single number is an estimate, and how sample size shapes how much to trust it
  • Why "regression testing" doesn't transfer cleanly to ML — shown by example
  • A map of the recurring sources of uncertainty we'll meet across the course
  • Why eval in software and in AI are different concepts (but, alas, share the name) and what this implies
Skills you unlock
  • Pick the right metric for your purpose
  • Pick the right baseline and tell whether a change is a real improvement, or noise
  • Build a test set you can trust — and know when you can't trust someone else's
  • Estimate the uncertainty around a result — give it as a range, not a point
  • Report on uncertainty in a manner that clarifies what we know and what we don't
  • Ask questions that uncover (but do not punish) uncertainty
  • Identify and avoid common errors
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Sampling noise — a score is one draw from a distribution. The same eval run again can land 5 points higher or lower. Sample size — and what's in the sample — set how much it wobbles.

L2
Assessing one "run" of an Agent · Part I

How we programmatically assess whether an AI answer is correct — programmatic checks, judges, and numeric scores. Ground Truth, AI Judges, and Judges of Judges.

Content
  • Programmatic checks for the "easy" cases — exact match, format and schema checks, JSON validation
  • When there is no clean ground truth — reference-based metrics, and LLM-as-judge
  • Score types: binary (0/1), multiclass, ordinal (1–5), continuous
  • Variance around a score — the same AI answer can be scored differently on re-run, even when the case has a clear answer
  • A first "judge of judges" — calibrating a judge against humans, sanity-checking the rubric
  • (Subjectivity and "the judge has opinions" is Part II, in L6)
Skills you unlock
  • Choose a scoring approach that fits the task — programmatic, reference-based, or LLM-as-judge
  • Write a clear rubric an LLM-as-judge can apply consistently
  • Spot when variance is coming from the judge, not from the data
  • Calibrate a judge against human labels before trusting it
  • Pick the right score type (binary, multiclass, ordinal, continuous) for the output
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Variance around a score — the wobble in the score for a given case, even when the case has a clear answer. The judge — programmatic, LLM, or human — is an instrument with its own noise. Distinct from L1's sampling noise, which comes from picking different cases.

L3
Proportions, Probabilities, Beliefs: What are we measuring?

A gentle introduction to probability and uncertainty.

Content
  • Proportion vs. probability vs. belief — three different things one number gets used for
  • The mean, median, and variance — reading a spread, not just a point
  • Random variables — and experiments as estimation of random variables
  • Bias vs. variance
  • Variability across domains, and why a sample may not stand for real traffic
  • The language we will use for the rest of the course
Skills you unlock
  • Say what claim a number is actually making — proportion, probability, or belief — and call out which one a teammate or vendor really meant
  • Quote a result as a range, not a point — give the spread, not just the mean
  • Tell when more data will fix your uncertainty problem and when it won't
  • Spot when a number is "right on average" but unreliable per-domain — and ask for the breakdown
  • Use a shared vocabulary so the same number means the same thing to everyone who sees it
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Variability across customers, domains, and time. Unlike sampling noise, this kind of spread does not shrink with more data — only better coverage does. The bias-vs-variance distinction lives here.

L4
Closed-Book Overfitting: Why "regression testing" generates biased results

Run an eval many times or watch many metrics and some will look good by pure chance. The sealed test set is the defense.

Content
  • "Regression testing" in the AI sense — why the same eval re-run shows different numbers
  • Multiple comparisons: with many metrics — and many re-tests — some will move by pure chance
  • The lucky-winner problem: best-of-N picked across many tries
  • Validation datasets — the train / validation / test split as the structural defense
  • Sealed test set: touched only to report, never to tune
  • Simpson's paradox in comparisons — an aggregate disagreeing with every per-domain story
Skills you unlock
  • Recognize a winner picked from too many tries — tell honest comparison from fishing
  • Decide which metrics matter up front, so the results land with weight
  • Set up a train / validation / test split that does its job
  • Keep a sealed test set — and notice when one is being touched during iteration
  • Spot Simpson's paradox in a comparison and ask for the per-domain breakdown
Uncertainty dimension mastered

MHT — bias from multiple hypothesis testing. Track many metrics or re-run many times and one will jump by pure chance. The sealed test set is the structural defense; statistical corrections are the numerical one.

L5
Open-Book Overfitting: The Plague of Modern AI Evals

Every AI team faces overfitting — to the prompt, to the benchmark, to the eval set. Spot it before it ships.

Content
  • Overfitting as the gap between the score you measured and behaviour in the wild
  • The three flavours of AI overfitting: model (memorized train / leaked test), prompt (tuned to the eval set), leaderboard (fishing — the MHT side, covered in L4)
  • Benchmark contamination — when the system has already seen the test
  • The structural defense (sealed test set, validation discipline) was set up in L4; here we focus on recognizing the symptom
Skills you unlock
  • Spot a score inflated by tuning against its own benchmark
  • Tell model overfitting from prompt overfitting from leaderboard overfitting
  • Recognize benchmark contamination — when a "win" is really memorization
  • Report the validation-vs-test gap so the inflation is visible, not hidden
  • Resist the institutional pressure to "improve the eval number"
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Overfitting — the optimistic gap between the measured score and behaviour in the wild. Grows every time the system, the prompt, or the team is tuned against the eval. In modern AI it shows up everywhere — at the model, the prompt, and the leaderboard.

L6
Subjectivity and Noise in Judges and Ground Truth

When there is no clean ground truth — when the "right answer" is contested, and the judge has its own opinions.

Content
  • When ground truth itself is contested — humans disagree on the right answer
  • Annotator / rater disagreement, and inter-rater agreement
  • LLM-as-judge has biases too: length bias, position bias, sycophancy, format preferences
  • Multiple raters and pooling judgments
  • Subjectivity is not bias to be "removed" — it's the reality of the task
  • A deeper "judge of judges" — auditing a judge for systematic errors
Skills you unlock
  • Tell when "no ground truth" is the actual situation, not a temporary gap
  • Set up multiple raters and read inter-rater agreement
  • Spot common LLM-judge biases — length, position, sycophancy
  • Push back on a single judge's score for a subjective task
  • Decide whether to fix the judge or to live with — and report — the subjectivity
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Subjectivity & judge errors. When the question has no single right answer, the score isn't just noisy — it's contested. Adding raters helps; pretending the judge is objective doesn't.

L7
Additional sources of errors and uncertainty — and why they compound

An eval is not run once — it has to keep meaning something as the system, model, and world move.

Content
  • Drift and model evolution — yesterday's eval does not automatically carry forward
  • Uncertainties compound — every source from L1–L6 stacks; the real uncertainty in a number is their combination
  • Re-baselining: when to redo the eval, and how to compare results across model versions
  • The eval-driven improvement loop: design, run, report, update, and re-check
Skills you unlock
  • Estimate the combined uncertainty of an eval — not just the largest single source
  • Re-baseline an eval on a schedule, and after every model or world change
  • Report design, method, and caveats so a result can be challenged honestly
  • Design an eval that survives the next quarter, not just the next release
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Drift, model evolution, and compounding. The eval ages as the world moves, and the earlier sources stack: the real uncertainty in a number is their combination, not the largest single one.

L8
Designing Reliable Experiments

Run the whole eval: from product question to experiment plan, measured result, decision report, and next iteration.

Content
  • Start with the decision: what would change if the experiment came back positive?
  • Turn a product claim into an experiment plan: baseline, candidate, population, metrics, and guardrails
  • Build the evaluation set: representative strata, dev / validation / sealed test split, and judge calibration set
  • Choose scoring instruments: programmatic checks where possible; calibrated LLM judge where judgment is required
  • Run the comparison: point estimate, range, per-domain breakdowns, judge reliability, and multiple-metric discipline
  • Write the decision report: what can ship, what cannot, what remains uncertain, and when to re-baseline
Skills you unlock
  • Translate a vague quality goal into a measurable experiment without losing the product decision
  • Design an eval plan that includes data, metrics, judges, holdouts, acceptance criteria, and reporting
  • Read an end-to-end result as evidence, not as a verdict handed down by one number
  • Spot the hidden blocker in a positive aggregate result: weak strata, judge disagreement, safety guardrails, drift, or overfitting
  • Produce a final eval report that a PM, engineer, and executive can all act on consistently
Uncertainty dimension mastered

Total experiment risk. All earlier sources are in play at once: sampling noise, representativeness, metric choice, judge noise, subjectivity, multiple testing, overfitting, drift, and model evolution.

OPTIONAL DEEP DIVE
The Gaussian & the Central Limit Theorem

Where statistics came from, and why the normal distribution keeps showing up — felt, not proven.

What we cover
  • A short history of statistics — the law of error, Galton & Pearson, Fisher and experiment design
  • The normal distribution demystified — not a law of nature, just what a pile of independent noise looks like once you add it up
  • The Central Limit Theorem, felt not proven — why one outcome isn't normal but an average drifts toward it
  • Why averages settle, and why a confidence interval has the shape it does
Where it connects
  • L1–L2 — measurement as experiment, the random variable
  • L3 — averages settling, noise around a mean
OPTIONAL DEEP DIVE
Conditional Probability & Bayes

What a probability means once you condition on evidence — and why "92% confident" is a promise the model may not keep.

What we cover
  • Conditional probability — "given what?", and the trap of reading an unconditional number as if it were conditional
  • Bayes' rule and base rates — why a 90%-accurate judge's flag can still be wrong most of the time (the prosecutor's fallacy)
  • Calibration — when "92% confident" really means right 92% of the time; the reliability diagram and ECE
  • Calibration ≠ accuracy; why modern LLMs skew overconfident; temperature scaling
  • The payoff — confidence-based routing and abstention only work if the model is calibrated
Where it connects
  • L1 — class imbalance / base rates, seen from the belief side
  • L2 — judge accuracy vs. "is a flagged answer truly bad?"
  • L3 — proportion / probability / belief, applied to the model's own self-report
