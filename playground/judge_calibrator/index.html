Pick your Judge

L2 lab · in L1 the judge was perfect. Now it has two defects — bias and noise — and they do different things to your number.

Uncertainty dimension: variance & offset around a score. Turn the dials; bootstrap the result.

Where L1 left off

Lesson 1 made two quiet assumptions: there is a perfect ground truth, and we can compare the AI's answer to it perfectly. The only uncertainty was which cases landed in your test set (sampling noise). This lesson removes the second assumption. The thing that scores an answer — the judge — is itself an instrument, and instruments are imperfect in two distinct ways.

Because gen-AI answers are rarely just right or wrong ("Paris" vs. "Paris, the City of Light" vs. "a city in France"), our judge gives a graded score — a quality rating, not a pass/fail stamp. That makes the two defects easy to separate.

Bias — systematic

The judge is consistently too generous or too harsh — every answer rated a few points high or a few points low, the same direction every time.

Effect: your measured score is shifted off the truth. More data does not fix it — it just makes you confidently wrong.

Noise — random

The judge is inconsistent: re-run it (temperature, an ambiguous rubric, a different annotator) and the same answer gets a different score.

Effect: your measured score wobbles around its average. It averages out with more judgments — but on a finite test set it widens the spread you must report.

Bootstrap: how sure are you about that number?

You ran the judge once and got a single mean score. How much would it move if your test set had been a different draw, and the noisy judge had rated differently? Bootstrap answers that without any formula: resample the test set with replacement thousands of times, re-score each resample, and look at the spread of the results. (It's the model-free cousin of L1's Beta interval, and it works for any score — binary, 1–5, or continuous.)

The asymmetry that matters. Raise noise → the bootstrap distribution gets wider: noise is honest, it shows up as a bigger interval you can report and then shrink (average more judgments, tighten the rubric, drop the temperature). Slide bias → the whole distribution shifts away from the green truth line, and the interval can sit entirely in the wrong place. The bootstrap only resamples what the judge said, so it cannot see the bias — it hands you a tight, confident interval around the wrong answer. Now push test-set size up with bias on: the interval gets narrower and excludes the truth even more decisively. More data cures noise; it cannot cure bias.

The L2 takeaway

  1. Bias and noise are different defects. Noise is random and averages out; bias is systematic and persists. Telling them apart is the first move.
  2. Both widen your real uncertainty — differently. Noise widens the interval you can measure. Bias adds an offset your interval can't even see, so the truth may lie outside a confident-looking CI.
  3. They call for different fixes. Reduce noise by averaging repeats, lowering temperature, and writing a crisper rubric. Reduce bias only by auditing the judge against trusted labels — calibrating a "judge of judges" (the thread we pick up in L6).
