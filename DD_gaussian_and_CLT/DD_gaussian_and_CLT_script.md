# Script

> Take-home deep dive — not lectured live. This is a reading-walkthrough rather than a talk script: the order to read in and the one idea to leave with.

## Reading path

1. **The problem is old.** Astronomers measuring the same planet got different numbers — the original "the measurement could have come out differently." Statistics was built to answer it.
2. **Variation becomes the subject.** From combining noisy observations (Gauss/Laplace) to studying the spread itself (Galton/Pearson) to designing experiments (Fisher).
3. **The normal distribution, demystified.** Not magic, not required — it's what you get when many small independent errors add up (the CLT).
4. **Back to evals.** One outcome isn't normal; the *average* over many cases drifts toward normal. That's why averages settle and intervals have their shape.

## The one takeaway

"Normal" = what a pile of independent noise looks like once you add it up. Nothing more mystical than that — and it's why averaging many noisy cases gives you something stable to reason about.
